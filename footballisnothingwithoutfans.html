Football counts for zero right now. This is a pandemic, a situation none of us had experienced before now. The priority is to concentrate on this fight, the rest does not matter."

Carlo Ancelotti, March 2020

For years we have taken football for granted, from the intense tactical battles to the pre-match pints in crowded pubs. For many football is not zero, it is their everything, and when Covid-19 came to England in early 2020 it all got taken away. The beautiful game was on the brink.

Professional football in England was suspended on March 13th 2020 due to a rising number of coronavirus cases. The Football Association initially announced a three-week postponement to ease the strain on clubs and to monitor the national situation, but this postponement went on to last for over three months.

Carlo Ancelotti, Everton manager at the time, endorsed the shutdown, saying: "We could not continue. Health is the most important thing for everyone: teams, fans, media, everyone who works in football."

The shutdown of football may have been the right decision, but it sparked a turbulent 16-month journey for fans across the country. With lockdown measures now eased and the new Premier League season around the corner, it is time to re-live that journey.

As Jock Stein and Matt Busby proclaimed decades ago: "Football is nothing without fans."

Timeline of Football during the Pandemic

March 13th 2020 - Fixtures postponed

The Premier League, English Football League, Women's Super League and Football Association united to postpone fixtures following rising Covid-19 cases, including Arsenal manager Mikel Arteta and Chelsea attacker Callum Hudson-Odoi.

March 26th 2020 - FA voids non-league seasons

The Football Association announced that all non-league and grassroots seasons below National League and National League North and South levels will be void as a result of Covid-19.

June 17th 2020 - Premier League resumes

The Premier League resumed with Aston Villa vs Sheffield United played behind closed doors. All matches were picked up by broadcasters to allow fans to watch games in the absence of attending stadiums. With the break in the season and cases of Covid-19 still prominent, the number of substitutes increased from 3 to 5.

August 1st 2020 - FA Cup final without fans

The FA Cup final was played at an empty Wembley Stadium for the first time ever as Arsenal won a record 14th FA Cup with a 2-1 victory over Chelsea.

August 29th 2020 - Pilot test event

2,500 fans were welcomed back to Brighton's Amex Stadium for their friendly match against Chelsea as a government test event.

September 12th 2020 - 2020/21 Premier League season begins

The 2020/21 Premier League season kicked off with stadiums remaining empty despite lockdown measures having eased in England. The 5-substitute rule continued to help squads navigate the short break in between seasons as well as Covid-19 outbreaks.

October 9th 2020 - Pay Per View announced

In the absence of fans in stadiums, it was announced that 5 of each matchday's 10 Premier League games would be moved onto Sly Sports and BT Sport's box office platforms. Each match cost £14.95 on the pay per view scheme which would help generate revenue for clubs in light of no matchday income.

November 13th 2020 - Pay Per View cancelled

Following uproar from football fans, the pay per view model was scrapped by the Premier League. Instead, all games were distributed between Sky Sports, BT Sport, BBC and Prime Video.

December 2nd 2020 - Fans return to stadiums

For the first time in 266 days, football fans returned to stadiums for competitive football. Cambridge, Carlisle, Charlton, Luton, Shrewsbury and Wycombe were the first to welcome fans back with a maximum capacity of 2,000.

December 19th 2020 - Tier 4 lockdown

The return of fans is short-lived with England entering into a Tier 4 lockdown amid rising Covid-19 cases.

April 18th 2021 - Fans at FA Cup Semi-Final

4,000 fans attended the FA Cup Semi-Final as Leicester City beat Southampton 1-0. The game was part of a pilot scheme with research being done about the easing of coronavirus restrictions.

April 18th 2021 - Founding of The Super League

Arsenal, Chelsea, Liverpool, Manchester City, Manchester United and Tottenham agree to join 6 other European clubs to form The Super League, chaired by Florentino Pérez. The league intended to rival, and eventually replace, the UEFA Champions League.

April 21st 2021 - 6 English clubs withdraw from The Super League

After only a matter of days, the 6 Premier League clubs announced their withdrawal from the European Super League following widespread backlash from the British government, footballing bodies, media and fans.

May 2nd 2021 - Manchester United vs Liverpool postponed

Thousands of fans from Manchester United gathered outside Old Trafford and the team hotel to protest against their owners, the Glazers. During the protests, hundreds of fans made their way into the stadium and onto the pitch, forcing the abandonment of the game, which was then played on May 13th.

May 18th-23rd 2021 - Fans return to Premier League stadiums

Fans return to Premier League stadiums for each team's final home game of the 2020/21 season. Capacity is limited to a maximum of 10,000 fans or 25% of stadium capacity depending on which is lower.

June 13th 2021 - Fans at Wembley for England's first game of Euro 2020

18,497 fans attended England's opening game of the European Championships at Wembley Stadium against Croatia.

July 11th 2021 - 67,173 at Wembley for Euro Final

67,173 fans were in attendance for the final of the European Championships between England and Italy. Strict entry requirements included negative Covid tests and proof of full vaccination, however a number of extra fans forced their way into the stadium.

The Super League

I see more a European league developing over time rather than one team going out of the country. The national leagues will survive but maybe in 10 years, you will have a European league."

Arsène Wenger, August 2009

Arsène Wenger predicted a European Super League over 10 years ago and on April 18th 2021 his prediction came true. Despite many expecting it, the announcement of the European Super League shocked the football world and country alike.

The owners of 12 European clubs, Arsenal, Chelsea, Liverpool, Manchester City, Manchester United and Tottenham alongside Atletico Madrid, Barcelona, Real Madrid, AC Milan, Inter Milan and Juventus, announced the formation of The Super League, a rival for the UEFA Champions League.

The announcement sparked a frenzy across England and Europe with UK Prime Minister Boris Johnson, the Premier League and UEFA all condemning its formation.

A Premier League statement on April 18th criticised The Super League, stating: "The Premier League condemns any proposal that attacks the principles of open competition and sporting merit which are at the heart of the domestic and European football pyramid."

"Fans of any club in England and across Europe can currently dream that their team may climb to the top and play against the best. We believe that the concept of a European Super League would destroy this dream."

The meritocracy of the football pyramid became a central argument against The Super League as it was announced that the founding members of the competition would be guaranteed entry, leaving only 5 spaces for teams to qualify based on achievements in the prior season.

The Format

The European Super League was to include 20 clubs: AC Milan, Arsenal, Atletico Madrid, Barcelona, Chelsea, Inter Milan, Juventus, Liverpool, Manchester City, Manchester United, Real Madrid, Tottenham, 3 further founding members and 5 teams who would qualify based on domestic performance over the previous season.

The 20 clubs would be split into 2 groups of 10 where each team would play each of the other teams in their group home and away. The top 3 teams from each group would progress into a playoff bracket at the quarter-final stage, whilst the 4th and 5th placed teams from each group would compete in a two-legged playoff game to decide the remaining quarter-finalists.

The quarter-finals and semi-finals would be played as two-legged ties before a single fixture final would be played at a neutral venue in order to determine the winner of The Super League.

The Justification

A press release from The Super League sought to explain why the league was formed, stating: "The formation of the Super League comes at a time when the global pandemic has accelerated the instability in the existing European football economic model.

Further, for a number of years, the Founding Clubs have had the objective of improving the quality and intensity of existing European competitions throughout each season, and of creating a format for top clubs and players to compete on a regular basis."

The 12 founding clubs had accumulated in excess of £4.3 billion of debt by the time The Super League was announced. A lack of matchday income due to no fans contributed significantly to the losses of these clubs whilst operating costs and wages remained high despite cuts being made.

In his prediction of a super league, Arsene Wenger referenced money as a driving force, saying: "The way we are going financially is that even the money that will be coming in from the Champions League will not be enough for some clubs because they spend too much money."

JP Morgan, a leading investment banking company, pledged £2.8 billion to the founding clubs to help launch The Super League, allowing for the rebuilding of infrastructure and recovery from the pandemic.

The reaction to The Super League from fans and media alike was overwhelmingly negative. News broke during Sky Sports' Super Sunday and BT Sport's FA Cup Semi-Final coverage, sparking a raw and emotional response from the pundits on live television.

One of the few not to instantly dismiss The Super League was Michael Cox of The Athletic, pointing out the flaws in the current system: "Use any measure you like - points totals, goal difference, titles, wage bills - and inspect the trajectory of major European leagues over the past two decades, and it becomes clear we are experiencing staggering, rampant inequality completely off the scale."

Bayern Munich have won 9 Bundesligas in a row, Juventus have won 9 of the last 10 Serie A titles, Paris Saint Germain have won Ligue Un 7 of the last 9 years, Barcelona have won 5 La Liga's in 10 years and Manchester City have 5 Premier League titles in the past decade.

While Cox's argument is well-founded, the sheer backlash from supporters in England became so intense that any logical justification of The Super League received little thought.

This same backlash helped to sway FIFA's stance on The Super League, which they had once backed following an approach from Spanish finance consortium A22 in 2019. The Super League required the support of FIFA to launch in April, and their U-turn helped seal the league's fate.

Ownership Protests

The uproar from football fans towards The Super League spilled over into deeper grievances for both Arsenal and Manchester United supporters. As a result, mass protests ensued at both clubs as supporters sought to voice their displeasure towards their owners.

Arsenal

Thousands gathered outside Emirates Stadium ahead of Arsenal vs Everton on April 23rd to speak out against Arsenal's owner Stan Kroenke.

Ian Wright, former Arsenal striker, gave his support for the protestors outside the Emirates, telling DAZN: "I stand with them."

"We are talking about a silent owner. Since he has been at our club he has not invested in our club properly. The way they buy the club, the way they serviced the debt with this club, the fact they wanted to go into this competition [The Super League], it is like they have given up on us."

A large group of supporters began the protest walking around the stadium holding up signs and lighting flares, with loud chants including "we want our Arsenal back" and "get out of our club."

The supporters eventually gathered outside the main entrance to the stadium in full voice.

During the protests, Daniel Ek, co-founder and CEO of Spotify, announced his interest to buy Arsenal Football Club. In the ensuing weeks, Daniel Ek submitted a formal offer to the Kroenke family, supported by consortium partners Thierry Henry, Dennis Bergkamp and Patrick Viera.

The offer was rejected as the Kroenke's 'don't need the money' but Ek insisted his offer would remain on the table should they ever want to sell.

Manchester United

Manchester United's game against Liverpool became the heart of a mass protest at Old Trafford on May 2nd with United fans calling for owners, the Glazers, to sell the club following their agreement to join The Super League.

Thousands of fans descended on Old Trafford chanting against the Glazers alongside banners and flares before a small number eventually breaking onto the pitch.

2 miles away, in the city centre, fans swarmed The Lowry Hotel where Manchester United players were staying, preventing them from leaving to their team bus to travel to the game.

Adam Blaize, a Manchester United fan at the protest, discussed his main issue with the owners, stating: "Manchester United were a debt-free club since 1931 and the Glazers bought them with loan payments and the debt has been rising."

"They will say there has been investment in the club in different areas but it has been poor investment" he added.

The impact of the protest was overshadowed somewhat by a small number of individuals carrying out acts of violence against police and staff.

One police officer suffered a slash to the face, almost damaging his eye. A man was arrested on suspicion of assault but has since been released on bail with Greater Manchester Police urging witnesses to come forward.

The game itself was eventually rearranged for the safety of the players and staff, being played on May 13th.

Impact of Fans on the pitch

Over the 2019/20 and 2020/21 seasons, Premier League teams who have played across both campaigns played between 30 and 35 games in front of fans, and between 41 and 46 games without fans.

The trend amongst Premier League teams has been a decrease in home performance without fans, and an increase in away performance.

Jonathan Rhodes, Sport Psychologist, explained the discrepancy between home form and away form in a typical season with fans, saying: "There is usually a home advantage because of an increase in motivation and thus effort due to increased arousal and mutual support."

Rhodes' suggestion as to why the difference between home and away performance has been altered over the past two seasons is that "players need anxiety and this has been limited in home matches and enhanced in away performance."

Emily Collett, a fellow Sport Psychologist, suggested that a factor in this change is "the crowd having a possible influence on refereeing" impacting both the home and away team.

She also highlighted the players' will to play in front of fans: "A lot of players this season have mentioned missing fans and cannot wait to perform in front of fans, and the change of atmosphere, as well as the other factors mentioned likely had a huge impact on performance as we have seen this season."

Summer and Beyond

Since the end of the Premier League season, the European Championships have taken England by storm.

Wembley Stadium hosted eight games in total including all but one of England's games en route to the final. Both Semi-Finals and the Final, between England and Italy, were played at Wembley with the final reaching a capacity of 67,173.

England fans at Wembley for the European Championships (Credit: Jacob Ranson)

Despite the increased fan attendance at Wembley representing a transition towards normality for England, the European Championship Final shone a negative light on the return of fans to sporting venues.

Thousands of fans forced their way into the stadium without a ticket, resulting in several stewards getting injured, whilst family members and guests of the two teams were abused and forced to leave their seats. In the process, Covid-19 checkpoints were breached as well as Italy fans' bio-secure bubble which allowed them to travel to the game.

Following the Euro Final, the Community Shield curtain raiser between Manchester City and Leicester City will be the next major football match to be played at Wembley, kicking off the 2021/22 season.

David Elleray was pleased for Wembley to host fans once again, stating: "We are delighted at the prospect of the 2021 FA Community Shield being played in a partially-full Wembley Stadium and, with this return to 'normality' match."

It has been a long journey for football fans, but the beautiful game is nearly back.
